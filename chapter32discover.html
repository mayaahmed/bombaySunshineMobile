Chapter 32

Discovering rural India

Tara soon began to travel to the interiors of Maharashtra with a group of students from the Indian University.

"It is a wonder how your parents let you travel now that you are older" said Seeta. They reminisced about the times when Tara's parents would not give permission for overnight trips.

"I have figured out a trick that works quite well" said Tara. "Instead of asking my father for permission to go somewhere, I just tell him I am going. Since he does not like to contradict me, this trick works quite well. Moreover for some reason they trust me now."

Traveling educated Tara like nothing else did. Every where she went she learned new things about people and places. In some of the villages Tara visited there were groups of huts built away from the main village. The huts were made of mud and leaves whereas the village houses are made of bricks and stones. A group of such huts formed a pada. This was where the tribal people lived. The tribal people were also called Adivasis which meant indigenous people. The tribals were considered lower castes and hence had to live away from the main village. The tribals had a primitive lifestyle and traditionally lived off the forests. After the forests were gone, they had taken to farming for the villagers.

Tara walked among the houses in the padas with other people from her group. Most houses did not have any doors. A lot of houses were empty.

"No one steals anything here" one Adivasi woman explained to Tara. "We hardly have anything."

"Why are these houses empty?" asked Tara.

"Now it is summer and the people have gone to the cities to work" explained the Adivasi woman. "They will come back during the rainy season when there is work here."

"There is no robbery here" said an old lady smiling. "We all help each other. If someone's house gets messed up in the rain, we help build their house. We are all friends here."

"In fact, if there is a wedding the groom's family feeds his guests and bride's family feeds her guests" said the first Adivasi woman. "Also the guests bring ingredients for the meal. We all help each other here."

"That is an interesting tradition compared to what we have in the city" said Tara. "In the city the bride's family has to face the burden of the wedding expenses. We have a lot to learn from you."

That statement made the women surrounding Tara very happy. They all smiled and invited her to their homes. Tara entered one house which was practically empty. But somehow it was warm, welcoming, very aesthetically pleasing, and relaxing to Tara.

"I like your house. It is so well kept. What is that fruit hanging on the wall?" asked Tara.

"It is from the forest. Taste it, it is sour" said the woman.

Tara couldn't eat much of the fruit and she made a wry face which made the women laugh.

"What is that on the floor?" asked Tara.

"That is a distillery" explained the woman of the house.

"What is a distillery?" asked Tara.

"That is how you make alcohol" said the woman. "If the police catches us, we have to pay fines. We have to do this secretly. Sometimes there are raids. Alcohol is a problem here. The men drink heavily."

Tara stayed in the house of a village Panchayat leader. When Tara got back to her host's house, she watched the women cook bakhris (bread) on firewood for sometime. Then she took her diary and went to the fields and sat on a mound of mud reflecting on the things she had seen and heard that day. Soon the sun set and the sky was a picture of bliss.

She wrote:

"It is so peaceful here. Man appears to be a part of nature. Man is meant to live like this with open spaces and soothing trees with nature providing adventure and not in crowded cities where you step out of your apartments only to go somewhere."

A little before dinner time the group of students gathered in a circle to discuss the experiences of the day.

"Let us talk about what we learned today" said Dharam. "We will take turns to tell each other what were our impressions of this day. Let me start with what I learned. I noticed that the people in the padas are uneducated and extremely poor. The men I heard spend all their income on alcohol intensifying the already existing poverty."

After Dharam the students spoke when it was their turn to speak.

"I noticed that the rural women work very hard" said Lata. "They do manual labor, they raise children, and also take care of their drunk men. The women here are exploited."

"I can sympathize with the men drinking in this place" said Jain. "I can sympathize with people who are manual laborers drinking alcohol. It is a hard life. Plowing the field, being undernourished, being the insulted and humiliated, and being underpaid is not easy. The men here drink to escape the pains which are both mental and physical."

"I noticed that the problems these poor people have are similar to the problems poor people have in Bombay" said Tara. "In fact these people have better homes. I have changed my opinion about the reservation policy of the government for scheduled tribes. There was a lot of hue and cry over having seats reserved in colleges and schools for scheduled tribes and castes last year. The protests involved self immolation by young people. At that time I was against the reservation policy too. I thought that everything should be purely merit based. But after coming here I noticed that all those special concessions the government has made for these people have not reached them at all. These people don't even know about their special rights. Those concessions are being exploited by a corrupt system."

"I agree" said Lata. "I think if people actually came and saw how the scheduled tribes lived the protests would be of a different kind. These people are taking no concessions. They live by the sweat of their brow."

And the group began to discuss about how to spread awareness and education till it was time for dinner. They were served bhakri, dried peas curry, rice, and lentils for dinner. Tara and some of her friends had never eaten bhakri which was made of jowar.

"Why do they make bhakri only in the villages?" asked Tara.

"I think it comes out better on fire wood" said Lata. "May be they do not have enough wheat and we have wheat in the city. I am not sure. But I know it is tougher to eat bhakris than rotis. But I like bhakris."

Tara watched Lata eat bhakri and she was pulling and tearing at it with her teeth.

"You look like a little wild animal tearing at its prey. Kind of cute the way you eat bhakris" said Tara laughing.

After dinner Tara sat out in the courtyard of the house she was living in.

"Isn't it nice that we can come and stay in this house for free?" asked Lata. "That is the nice part of rural India where the homes are welcoming. Here in rural India one understands what people mean when they talk about traditional Indian hospitality."

"True" said Tara. "Look at the sky. Without the street lights the stars are so bright."

Dharam explained the constellations to them.

"The inverted V you see there is the constellation Taurus. The R shaped formation is Orion ..."

After some time he asked

"Tell me what is that sideways M Tara?"

"I wouldn't know" said Tara completely embarrassed that she had forgotten what Dharam had called it.

"It is Cassiopeia" explained an exasperated Dharam. He then went on to name a few more constellations.

Later that night Tara wrote in her diary

"This little group of people are dreamers like me. We dream of an ideal world and we are willing to work towards it. Our methods maybe sloppy or maybe it will get us nowhere. But we are not afraid to try and we do not care if it does not work. But we definitely will give it a shot."

Tara looked around the room she was sleeping in. The walls were made of mud and the floor was smeared with cow dung. She continued writing

"In the morning I will wake up to the chirping of birds and the sound of the women sweeping the yard. Both melodious sounds in its own way greeting the sunrise. This is when you are in harmony with existence and life is beautiful and stands still for some time."

Tara closed the diary and watched the flickering light from the lamp in a corner of the room for sometime and then she shut her eyes to listen to the sounds of the night. When Tara woke up in the morning, it was very cold. She went to the kitchen and sat near the fire. The woman of the house offered her a cup of tea and that made her warm.

"It gets real cold here. In Bombay it is never cold. But this tea took care of the cold" said Tara.

Tara stepped out into the yard and she watched a woman feeding the chicken grains.

"Have you noticed that the sounds of the morning are rather busy sounds? The chickens act as though they have a lot of important things to do and eat their breakfast in a hurry" said Lata joining Tara.

"Yes" said Tara. "It is kind of busy in the mornings. The sounds are all breakfast sounds. The cow eating grass and drinking water, the chicken eating grains, and the birds eating worms. Yuck."

"The chicken eat worms too. Double yuck" said Lata.

Tara and Lata took a walk along the fields and they stopped when they saw a stream. Women were washing clothes at the stream and they hit the clothes against the rocks as they washed.

"Where are you from?" asked one woman.

"We are from Bombay" said Lata. Lata then explained that they were visiting the village with a group of students from the Indian University.

"There is a wedding this evening. Will you two come for it?" asked the woman.

"Sure" said Tara and got the details of where the wedding was going to be.

"Come with all your friends. It is a rich man's wedding. There will be horses and a baraat. You will like it very much."

So that evening Tara and her friends went to the wedding.

"Since we are from the boy's side, we will take a baraat and go to the bride's house" said one woman. "When we go in the baraat you girls must dance."

Tara and Lata looked at each other a little shocked at what they heard and they of course refused to dance. They joined the baraat. The bridegroom was seated on a horse and the guests just walked along. Soon a group of little girls going along in the baraat insisted that Tara and Lata dance.

"Didi. Didi. Please dance" they pleaded.

"No. No" said Tara.

"But everyone dances. In a baraat people have to dance. We will all dance" said the little girls.

"So why don't you?" asked Tara.

"First you start didi" said the little girls.

Not wanting to disappoint the little girls Tara finally said

"Okay I will sing a song" and the little girls cheered up.

But to Tara's surprise, the news spread like wild fire. The baraat stopped completely and the next instant there was a mike in Tara's hand and all eyes were on her. Now she could not back out and she sang

"Zindagi jab bhi teri basm mein laati hai hume, yeh zamin chand se behatar nazar aati hai hume... (Whenever life brings me among your midst, this world looks better than the moon to me)."

But what Tara did that day was not an everyday event. It was not every day that some girl sang in a baraat in the middle of the village street and Tara realized it too late. But the little girls were very happy.

"Now you girls will dance wont you?" asked Tara.

"No" said the little girls.

"But..."

"We just said that to make you dance. After all since you are from Bombay you can do anything" said one girl.

"Boy! You kids tricked me into doing something stupid" said Tara.

"We are happy and proud that we had someone from Bombay singing in our baraat. See it was video taped" pointed out a little girl.

"It is done now" said Lata smiling.

"See the bhaus from Bombay are dancing" said the little girls happily.

Sure enough Tara could see some of the boys from her group dancing.

"They must have been fooled too like us" said Tara laughing.

The next place Tara went was to a village where people used small dams for irrigation. The Indian government spend a lot of money on irrigation by building big dams. The little village proved beyond doubt that the alternative method of small dams that trapped rain water in the soil was much better than redirecting large rivers from their natural path. The village was a success story. It was a tedious bus journey to the village that lasted for more than eight hours. It was hot and dry and very uncomfortable. But the sight of a green village after such a long spell of desert like scenery cheered Tara up.

"It is amazing how this little village in rural Maharashtra can be so green when all the villages around are so dry and ugly" said Tara.

"The idea of small dams was implemented by a social worker called Anna" said Dharam. "We will meet him in this trip. He is staying in the school hostel."

"School hostel?" asked Tara.

"This school was built by Anna too" said Dharam. "One person can make a lot of difference. Anna has changed the life of a whole village and beyond. This village stands out as an example to the world."

The school had become a reputed school. People from other villages send their children here to study. The villagers were very proud of their school. The little group from the Indian university were given rooms in the school hostel. Tara and her group sat down to eat lunch in the lunch area for school children. They were served a simple meal of rotis, daal, potato sabji, and rice. Tara enjoyed the rustic meal which had only basic spices. Finally she saw Anna. He came quietly and sat at one of the benches and ate the same meal the students ate. He seemed to look at no one. He was the sort of person used to having people gape at him. People came from all over the country to meet him.

"Anna, we are here" said Dharam to Anna. Dharam had already contacted Anna and informed him about their visit.

"Welcome, welcome. Hope you have a pleasant stay" Anna said beaming a smile.

After lunch Anna gave a speech about irrigation with small dams.

"These dams that we built here are built with money from the villagers. We needed no loans from outside. We pooled this money because we believed in the future. All those who contributed got back their money when their crops did well and they had enough water. We are living the dream of Gandhiji of a self sufficient village. I hope other villages follow our example."

"But not every village has a Anna" someone said and that made Anna smile.

"This is a strategy of irrigation that works" said Anna. "The rain water before it goes to the sea must be stopped. It is a simple concept and is part of the Indian irrigation system for a long time. The kings used to built tanks to store water. But nowadays people ignore these kind of irrigation systems. They want to bring high technology and they forget to make use of what is available first. Let me extend my welcome to all the visitors to our village again. Thank you for coming. Please feel free to go and look around the village. Welcome."

Anna then left. They did not see him again. That day the group walked around the village and talked to the villagers. Later in the evening Dharam talked to the group about how big dams created problems. People were dislocated from their ancestral lands. Big dams caused earthquakes and deforestation.

When Tara got back to her hostel she fortified herself with books on the big dams controversy. She poured through the books for a few days and nights. And Tara decided that big dams are evil. When Tara went home for the weekend, she pointed out an article about people protesting against big dams in the newspaper to her father and said

"Papa, after my trip I know that we do not need big dams for irrigation. Why is the government so insistent on building big dams even when there are so many protests?"

Tara then went on to describe what she had read about the evils of big dams. Tara's Military uncle was present and he gave a roaring laughter and said

"You may feel bad about people getting dislocated but let me ask you one question. You must answer honestly. You live in Bombay and we have rare power cuts. We are lucky. If you go to other cities there are frequent power cuts. Do you know why we have all this electricity? It is because of the big dams. Now tell me, if you had frequent power cuts would you like it?"

"No. I would hate it" said Tara.

"So let us not be hypocrites. We all know that cities need electricity and hence big dams" said Military uncle.

Tara did not have a reply for it and she only said

"I need to think about this. This is a new thought. I hate it that you put this view point and confused me. But I guess now I will have to sort out whether big dams are evil or not."

Tara went out of the house to the veranda and she stood there for sometime pondering about big dams.

"Sabji walla" shouted the vegetable seller. He came every evening to sell vegetables and he lingered a little below each building. He looked up and saw Tara and he smiled and asked

"Do you need any sabji?"

"I don't know. I will ask mother" said Tara.

Tara's mother said

"Yes, go get some coriander leaves for one rupee. I forgot all about it when I went to the market."

So Tara went down to buy the coriander leaves. It was a narrow road and the hand cart had hogged up almost all the road. One car wanted to pass and the vegetable seller seeing the car gave a sheepish grin and said

"Ek minute bhai sahab (One minute please)."

He then moved the cart to an edge of the road and the car passed slowly by. The vegetable seller then continued selling his vegetables again.

"That is it. I have it" said Tara to herself. It was her Eureka moment. She rushed home with the coriander leaves and told Military uncle

"We need both the big dams and the small dams. There should be no conflict here. The conflict is caused by vested interests. Just because the government is building big dams need not mean they should not build small dams. Just because the small dams are good need not mean that big dams are not useful. For some reason big dams and small dams are pitted against each other when they could easily exist together."

"You have something there. Write that down" said Military uncle laughing.

"I figured this out from the sabji walla" said Tara.

"How on earth did the sabji walla tell you all this?" asked a surprised Military uncle but Tara did not bother to explain.
