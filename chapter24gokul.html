Chapter 24

The Dahi hundi

Tara suddenly saw Manoj's face outside her window and she screamed in fright.

"It is only me"said Manoj. "Why are you screaming? You gave me such a fright. A fall from this floor means certain death. I am hanging up the Dahi hundi (pot of curds)."

Manoj and a few other boys tied the dahi hundi and several rupee notes on a rope decorated with flower garlands, fruits, and beetle leaves. They hung it high with one end tied to an iron rod attached to Tara's window and the other to a rod attached to an opposite flat's window. The pot hung thus in suspension high up in the air.

"I forgot it was the Gokulashtami festival tomorrow" said Tara.

Gokulashtami was a festival to celebrate God Krishna's birthday. Krishna as a child used to break pots to eat makkan (white butter). Therefore people hung up the dahi hundi for Govindas to break. The Govindas were a group of young boys who went from one building to the other and formed human pyramids to break the dahi hundi. Then they would collect the money tied on the decorated rope as prize. The fruits would be cut up and distributed among the audience as prasad.

"Isn't it kind of too high? Will the Govindas be able to get to it?" asked Tara.

"We have collected five hundred rupees this time"said Manoj. "That is a lot of money. Hence we are making it more challenging for the Govindas this time."

The next day morning the loudspeakers blared Hindi movie songs and there was a festive mood in the air. People kept watch for the Govindas to arrive. Tara and her friends stood all morning on the common veranda.

Many groups of Govindas came to Tara's building all morning. They always arrived amidst drum beats, singing, dancing, and a lot of yelling. The Govindas invariably sang "Govinda ala re (which meant Govinda is here in Marathi)."

But most of the groups looked at the pot and decided to give it a miss because it was hung too high. Some groups attempted to reach the dahi hundi but failed and went away. When finally a particularly large group of Govindas turned up Tara said

"I think this group will succeed."

"That guy in a black shirt is the main Govinda" said Seeta.

The guy in the black shirt looked around and his eyes rested on Tara and her friends. He gave a little salute, a slight smile, and then bounced around like a sports star.

"I think he is the main Govinda too"said Tara. "He acts like the hero of the day."

In a short while the group of boys formed a very tall human pyramid. People threw buckets of colored water on them. The idea was to make it as difficult as possible for the Govindas to break the pot. They fell down a few times and finally the main Govinda climbed high and broke the pot of curds with a stick. Then the rope was lowered and the Govindas collected the money. As they left the main Govinda gave another salute to Tara and her friends.

"I guess he thinks can afford to salute girls and get away with it in front of so many people today since he broke the dahi hundi" said Seeta.

"He is a daredevil" said Tara. "I am sure that is his style about everything in life."

"You flipped for him?" asked Seeta.

"Just for his courage" said Tara laughing.

"I like the tongue in cheek aspect about this festival" said Seeta.

As pots of curds broke around the city of Bombay in celebration of a festival poor people were losing their dwellings quite close to Tara's house. The Municipality was clearing the huts which bordered the colony maidan. When Tara heard about it she and her friends went to the terrace to watch. A crowd had already gathered on the terrace.

"Good thing they are breaking the huts"said Seema Akka loudly. "That maidan is for children to play in. Those huts are brewing centers for all social evil."

"Where was the police when they first build these huts illegally?'' asked Seeta. "These huts have been here forever now. What is the use? They will build it again tomorrow. All this is just fooling the public."

Tara saw Sarita among the crowd of slum dwellers . She was helping the slum dwellers salvage whatever they could before the bulldozers razed their little homes. Sarita was talking to the Municipality workers and bargaining for time for the slum dwellers. Tara and her friends went and hung around Sarita.

"Be quick." shouted a municipality worker. "In ten minutes we will start the bulldozer."

Tara saw the slum dwellers gather up their belongings and putting it a little away from their huts. After some time the huts were razed to the ground. Tara saw some women crying silently. The poor people were clearly depressed. Their hard earned money razed to the ground. But they had no where to go. They waited and watched and shortly after the bulldozers left they started making their little huts again with whatever material they could use.

"What is the point?"asked Tara. "Is this a sensible thing to do. I know we do not like our maidans to be bordered by huts. Either they should not let the first hut come up there or they should not do this. I do not understand. Is this the solution society has come up with to combat poverty? I do not understand."

"The reason we have to break these huts is that otherwise these structures will become permanent" said Sarita. "The rich will take over with the prices of land so high in Bombay. At the same time the society has a heart which will not shoot poor people to death nor does it want them to go homeless. So somewhere this is a compromise."

"Do you think that this is the best solution?" asked Seeta.

"In this game there are no good solutions" said Sarita. "None that I know of yet. All we can do is try to help as much as we can in the way we know best."

Late in the evening when Tara went to the terrace she saw the poor people were all settled again. She could see the smoke that came from the firewood the poor women used to cook their dinner.

Tara wrote down in her diary

"That smoke that emanates from the slums signifies the continuation of the drama of life. Till the end, it is going to be like this. A lot of churning of emotions and wringings of the heart and also a lot of mindless laughter at times. But each day goes to the next unrelenting in the most mundane fashion no matter what happens each day till the life's end."
